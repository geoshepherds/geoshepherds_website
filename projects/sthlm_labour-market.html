Stockholm - Labour Market

Visualising supply and demand of jobs
overview

For a presentation at Arbetsförmedlingen in May this year, we used part of their open data to visualise the relationship between job seekers and jobs available in Stockholm region. The aim of the visualisation was to illustrate some of their data in an alternative way to how it was, and currently is, being communicated.

The results are encoded in colour and transparency as a choropleth map. A green colour (horizontal axis) represents a low percentage of job seekers and a blue colour represents a higher percentage of job seekers. A light shaded colour (vertical axis) indicates that there are fewer jobs available and a dark shaded colour indicates that there are more jobs available.

methods
  1. Download data on the available jobs and job seekers within each kommun in Stockholm region for each week of 2015. The data required for this is openly available from Arbetsföremedlingen and can be found here.
  2. Download the working age (15-74) population data of each kommun in Stockholm region. This data is available from the openly accessible statistics database from SCB here.
  3. As the goal was to highlight the relationship between job seekers and available jobs on a choropleth map, we needed to normalise the data. A percentage was calculated for both the job seekers and the jobs available based on the data. Firstly, the percentage for job seekers was calculated based upon the working age population for each kommun. Secondly, the percentage for jobs available was calculated based upon the number of people looking for work.
  4. The data was mapped for interactive viewing in the browser using D3.js with a time-slider to display the results for each week of 2015.
results

The results we found showed some variance across the regions in Stockholm during 2015. As a general overview, there were more jobs available throughout the whole region in the months leading up to summer (March - May) than at other times of the year. This was especially noticeable in the kommuner surrounding Stockholm kommun where the city is situated. This could be attributed to an increase in seasonal job openings across the region.

On an individual kommun level, most display a light shaded green colour indicating that while there are a low percentage of the working population seeking work, there are not many jobs available for them within the kommun they live. Some kommuner that stand out are Danderyd and Södertälje. Danderyd shows a low percentage of their population looking for work and high number of jobs available throughout the year. Conversely, Södertälje consistently shows a higher percentage of people looking for work compared with there being fewer jobs available.

conclusion

By displaying the data from Arbetsförmedlingen on a map, we are able to gain a greater understanding of the similarities and differences geographically among the kommuner in Stockholm region. Through this, we can identify areas where a kommun is able to meet the demand of jobs from the people looking for work.

Identifying seasonal trends in the supply and demand of jobs required data with a high temporal frequency for the year. With the weekly statistics from Arbetsförmedlingen we were able to achieve this on a kommun level. However, there are limitations to mapping this type of data to a coarse geographic extent such as kommun. We lose spatial detail that show variations on a town or work-zone, and this is especially so in more remote kommuner with lower populations. To improve upon the spatial accuracy of this project the data supplied from Arbetsförmedlingen would be at a point level. Where the data for weekly statistics would be available for each Arbetsförmedlingen office.

To build on this visualisation further, having access to annual data over a longer period of time from Arbetsförmedlingen. This way, we could visualise long-term trends and identify potential influences on the labour market such as the global financial crisis of 2007 - 2008.
